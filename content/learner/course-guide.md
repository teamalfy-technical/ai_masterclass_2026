# Course Guide

## Course
Modern AI in Practice: Foundations, Codex, and OpenClaw

## Audience
- Primary audience: executives, product leaders, technical managers, engineering leads, innovation leads, and transformation sponsors making AI adoption decisions.
- Secondary audience: senior practitioners who need an operating view of AI systems without turning the course into a deep engineering build workshop.

## Prerequisites
- No coding is required.
- Participants should understand how their teams currently deliver work, approve changes, and manage operational risk.
- Each participant or table group should bring one workflow candidate they may want to test as an AI pilot.

## Delivery Mode
- Two days.
- Approximately 8 contact hours, with break placement managed by the facilitator.
- Demo-led, discussion-heavy, and executive-friendly.
- Best delivered with a shared learner pack, live walkthroughs, and structured table exercises.

## What This Course Is
- A practical course on modern AI systems, context engineering, coding agents, orchestration, and safe pilot design.
- A decision-making course for leaders who need a grounded language for evaluating AI usefulness, control boundaries, and rollout readiness.

## What This Course Is Not
- Not a model-comparison survey.
- Not a research seminar on how models are trained.
- Not a deep implementation workshop focused on shipping production code during the session.

## Course Arc

### Day 1: Foundations and Codex
- AI Fundamentals and the LLM Shift - 50 minutes - build a plain-language mental model of AI, large language models, strengths, and failure modes.
- AI Landscape and Systems Model - 40 minutes - distinguish model, workflow, retrieval, automation, and orchestration so teams place use cases correctly.
- Context Engineering and Prompting - 90 minutes - turn vague asks into bounded task briefs with context, constraints, and clear definitions of done.
- Codex Workflows, Validation, and Governance - 95 minutes - learn the inspect, plan, implement, validate, review loop and where approvals, skills, MCP, and AGENTS.md fit.

### Day 2: Systems and OpenClaw
- AI Systems, RAG, Tools, Memory, and Evaluation - 90 minutes - move from one-off prompting to grounded, measurable systems thinking.
- OpenClaw Orchestration, Skills, and Security - 90 minutes - understand Gateway, sessions, channels, tools, skills, plugins, routing, and safe defaults.
- Capstone Pilot Blueprint - 35 minutes - leave with one owned, measurable, and governable pilot idea.

## Learning Outcomes
By the end of the course, participants should be able to:
- explain what an LLM is, what it is good at, and where it fails without grounding or review;
- distinguish between a model, a workflow, and a governed AI system;
- write or review stronger task briefs with context, constraints, and acceptance criteria;
- describe the Codex operating loop of inspect, plan, implement, validate, review, and summarize;
- identify where AGENTS.md, MCP, skills, sandboxing, and approvals improve Codex practice;
- explain when OpenClaw is useful as an orchestration layer and when a simpler approach is enough;
- complete a pilot blueprint with users, controls, success metrics, and next steps.

## Participant Outputs
- One completed exercise workbook.
- One reviewed set of module summaries for post-session reference.
- One completed pilot blueprint for a realistic AI workflow candidate.
- One resource sheet of official docs and practical follow-up references.

## Governance Thread
Throughout the course, participants repeatedly return to five questions:
- What work should not be automated?
- What data is allowed into the system?
- What actions need human approval?
- How will success be measured?
- How will the pilot be paused, contained, or stopped if it fails?

## How To Use This Learner Pack
- Read this course guide before the session so the pacing and objectives are clear.
- Use the module summaries during breaks and after the course as a compact review set.
- Complete each workbook activity in sequence because the exercises build toward the pilot blueprint.
- Keep the resource sheet nearby during follow-up planning so the team uses official docs rather than memory alone.
